Evolution by Natural Selection

Requirements for Evolution

1. Phenotypic Variation

Observable differences in traits among individuals in a population. Without variation, no evolution can occur.

2. Differential Survival/Reproduction

Some variants survive and reproduce better than others. This creates selection pressure.

3. Heritability

Traits must have a genetic component that can be passed to offspring.

VPhenotypic = VGenetic + VEnvironment + 2Cov(G,E)

Where phenotypic variance is partitioned into genetic, environmental, and gene-environment interaction components

VGenetic = VAdditive + VDominance + VEpistasis

Genetic variance includes additive effects, dominance, and gene interactions

h² = VAdditive / VPhenotypic

Heritability - proportion of phenotypic variance due to additive genetic effects

Classic Example: Industrial Melanism in Biston betularia

Population Genetics

Genetic Framework

Population genetics examines the distribution and change of allele frequencies in populations. For the peppered moth example, we consider a single gene with two alleles:

Genotype Phenotype Frequency Fitness
AA Dark (carbonaria) p² w₁
Aa Dark (carbonaria) 2pq w₂
aa Light (typica) q² w₃
p + q = 1

Where p = frequency of A allele (dark), q = frequency of a allele (light)

Dominance Patterns

Complete Dominance

Heterozygotes have the same phenotype as one homozygote. In peppered moths, the carbonaria allele is completely dominant.

w₁ = w₂ = 1.0, w₃ = 0.1

Partial Dominance

Heterozygotes show intermediate fitness between homozygotes.

w₁ = 1.0, w₂ = 0.75, w₃ = 0.1

Overdominance

Heterozygotes have higher fitness than either homozygote, maintaining genetic diversity.

w₁ = 0.3, w₂ = 1.0, w₃ = 0.3

Quantitative Genetics

The Breeder's Equation

For continuously varying traits controlled by many genes, the response to selection is predicted by the breeder's equation:

R = h²S

Where:

R = Response to selection (change in mean trait)

h² = Heritability (proportion of variance due to additive genetic effects)

S = Selection differential (difference in mean before vs. after selection)

Example: Flower Size Evolution

If flowers in a population have a mean size of 10mm, and after selection the breeding individuals have a mean size of 12mm (S = 2mm), and heritability is 0.6, then:

R = 0.6 × 2mm = 1.2mm

The next generation will have a mean flower size of 11.2mm

Types of Selection

Selection Gradients vs. Selection Differentials

Selection Differential

The change in mean trait value following selection. Measures the strength of selection.

S = z̄after - z̄before

Selection Gradient

The relationship between fitness and the trait. Measures the mechanism of selection.

β = ∂ln(w)/∂z

Selection Patterns

Directional Selection

Favors one extreme of the trait distribution. Linear fitness-trait relationship.

Fitness ∝ Trait Value

Stabilizing Selection

Favors intermediate trait values. Quadratic fitness function with maximum at optimum.

Fitness ∝ -(Trait - Optimum)²

Disruptive Selection

Favors extreme trait values. U-shaped fitness function.

Fitness ∝ (Trait - Mean)²

Forces of Evolution

Beyond Natural Selection

Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequencies, especially important in small populations.

Variance in Δp ∝ 1/Ne

Mutation

Introduces new genetic variation at rate μ per generation.

Δp = μ(1-p) - νp

Migration

Gene flow between populations at rate m per generation.

Δp = m(pm - p)

Fitness Components

Total Fitness Decomposition

Total fitness can be broken down into multiplicative components:

W = Mating Success × Fertility per Mate × Offspring Survival

Understanding which component drives selection helps identify the mechanism

Example: Bullfrog Mating Success

In bullfrogs, body size affects multiple fitness components:

  • Sexual Selection: Larger males attract more mates
  • Natural Selection: Intermediate-sized males have higher survival
  • Fertility: Size may affect sperm production or egg fertilization success

Sexual vs. Natural Selection

Sexual Selection

Selection for traits that enhance mating success, even if they reduce survival.

wsexual = Number of Mates Obtained

Natural Selection

Selection for traits that enhance survival and general reproductive success.

wnatural = Survival × Fecundity
